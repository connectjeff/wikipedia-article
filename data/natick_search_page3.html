Natick Spring Annual Town Meeting night #4 (finale): bylaws updated; stabilization fund changes rejected

May 9, 2025 by Bob Brown

Natick Spring Annual Town Meeting 2025 dissolved on Thursday, May 8 after the town’s legislative body tackled just a few last articles, with the focus on bylaw updates and a citizen petition regarding stabilization funds. The latter took up the last half of the session during a meeting that ran close to three hours (see Natick Pegasus recording to review the whole meeting).

As is tradition, the moderator thanked the clerk, Natick Pegasus, and others for their support in making the meeting run smoothly. Moderator Jeff Alderson and previous moderator Frank Foss showed mutual respect for one another, with Alderson thanking Foss in helping him prep for the meeting, and Foss giving Alderson an attaboy that the rest of Town Meeting echoed.

Phew, Town Meeting is done. If you appreciated our coverage, please support our work with a tax-deductible donation

Charter & Bylaw Review Committee update

The Charter & Bylaw Review Committee (CBRC) has been going over the town charter (aka, constitution) and bylaws over the past three years, and expects to wrap things up at Fall Annual Town Meeting. CBRCs are formed every five years to do their review and issue a report, per the Natick Home Rule Charter.

Committee Secretary Christine Weithman shared highlights from the report under Article 23, and the news was good. “I am happy to report we are 95% done with our work,” she said. “Don’t groan. I do believe the work we bring back in the fall will be very quick…”

Previously Natick Home Rule Charter changes approved by Town Meeting are expected this month to gain the state’s blessing.

Weithman also previewed motions under Article 24, which included a handful of motions regarding bylaw changes. Two of these will be back at Fall Annual Town Meeting. Two focused on updating language about the town’s tax collector, including the elimination of a requirement to post a list of those behind on taxes in an old fashioned newspaper. Now the town can publish that information “in a manner determined by the town administrator.” Oh, like on Natick Report?

We also learned that copy editing skills are alive and well in Natick, as Town Meeting approved four motions under Article 24, but not before channeling their grammar teachers within the confines of Kennedy Middle School, where the session was held. Various wording changes were made before motions were approved.

Citizen petition on stabilization funds

Article 25, the last article on the warrant to be discussed at this Town Meeting, was submitted by Paul Griesmer, Rick Jennett et. al., and was presented by Griesmer. The article included two motions, one about the capital stabilization fund and the other about the operating stabilization fund, both of which were established in 2010 and 2011, respectively.

Griesmer said it was learned recently that these funds were restricted, unlike what town counsel said in 2011. They could be used for any lawful municipal purpose with a two-thirds Town Meeting vote, Griesmer quoted the counsel as saying back then. Updates to state rules have put further restrictions on the funds, though Griesmer said there are still complicated ways to access stabilization funds for purposes other than those originally designated.

The motions under Article 25 were designed to re-emphasize the notion that monies in these funds could be used for “any lawful municipal purpose.” Griesmer said making these changes won’t harm the town’s bond rating, as some have suggested.

“No one advocates draining reserves for any old purpose,” he said. “But when you have an emergency, when you have a tight timeframe [say the federal administration quashes $1.8M in school grants…] I don’t want to see two-thirds of Town Meeting have its hands tied to be able to rescue an operating department to cushion a blow…”

Griesmer emphasized the article sponsors aren’t advocate that any money be taken out of the funds, but rather, that the restrictions be removed.

The Finance Committee voted favorable action on both motions under Article 25, though during discussion at Town Meeting, FinCom Chair Todd Gillenwater said he would change his favorable vote to unfavorable based on his updated understanding of the situation. Following Griesmer’s presentation at Town Meeting, Gillenwater sought town counsel clarification on whether it was true that Town Meeting could by two-thirds vote change the restriction on a fund and then by another two-thirds vote approve an appropriation, and was told “yes.” FinCom Secretary Cody Jacobs (was missed the FinCom meeting reviewing this article) also argued against the motions, noting that Town Meeting already has the power to access the stabilization funds as needed.

Following Griesmer’s presentation, Town Meeting member and Select Board member Richard Sidney rose to recommend indefinite postponement of the article as a whole. He continued by saying part of the town’s financial success, including AAA bond ratings, is due to its strong reserves. Natick’s reserves position helps keep its borrowing rates low, he said, citing correspondence with the town’s financial director (see memo from finance director).

Sidney added that the town is in the midst of reviewing its financial principles to ensure they are in line with best practices.

“In my opinion, the town is well served by keeping these funds restricted, as initially passed by Town Meeting,” Sidney said, in encouraging Town Meeting to vote for indefinite postponement. As reported by Natick Report in April, the Select Board voted for indefinite postponement at one of its meetings.

Among other Town Meeting members against the motions was Jonathan Freedman, a former FinCom and Select Board member. He agreed with earlier statements about how Town Meeting already has a way to let the town tap into stabilization funds as needed. He also said: “I think it is very important that we do not look at our stabilization funds in isolation. I think it’s very important that we look at them as part of a process, as part of a system. As they are constituted right now, they are structured as part of a system of checks and balances, and part of a very important planning process…”

Town Meeting member Carol Gloff argued against the motions, in part citing faith in the ability of town department leaders to work together on financial issues ahead of Town Meeting to come up with compromises, even during a difficult override year. If things can’t be sorted out, there’s always Town Meeting, and that the stabilization funds don’t need to be redefined to let Town Meeting do its work, she said.

Town Meeting member Foss recommended voting against indefinite postponement but for the main motions, and made a plea that Natick “get the financial heads of our town to clear this up, to put together a plan for how we can move and be nimble to use these funds when circumstances like these catastrophes come up.”

Member Julian Munnich voted in favor of motions A and B, calling the proposal “quite frankly, honesty, truth in advertising, truth in labeling of our funds, and I just don’t see how that can be bad. They’re not restricted, they’re allocated, they’re assigned, and let us be truthful about how they are expended.”

Town Meeting did not vote for indefinite postponement of Article 25. But it did vote down both motions by totals of 29-81-0 (capital) and 25-83-1 (operational).

Town Meeting member feedback survey

Town Meeting members are invited by the moderator to fill out a feedback survey about how Town Meeting went.

 

Restocking the Finance Committee

With the end of Spring Annual Town Meeting, Five Finance Committee member terms will be expiring at the end of June. The Finance Committee plays a huge role in vetting articles ahead of Town Meetings, and summarizing its members’ takes on the motions under those articles.

If you’re interested in volunteering to become a member, fill out the application online. The moderator interviews and appoints new members.

Natick Spring Annual Town Meeting night #3: Zoning articles on floodplain maps, body art, ADUs & indoor rec

May 8, 2025 by Bob Brown

Night #3 of Natick Spring Annual Town Meeting got off to a flying start on May 6, with members voting almost unanimously on the first few motions with barely a comment. Moderator Jeff Alderson even referenced the Kentucky Derby-like record pace (See the whole meeting recording on Natick Pegasus).

Then Town Meeting got to the zoning articles… hopefully no one bet on many of these horses. Though in the grand scheme of past Town Meeting articles, these moved along relatively quickly, too.

Phew! Recapping Town Meeting is no simple task. Please consider supporting your dedicated Natick news source with a tax-deductible donation

Town Meeting made short work of Article 16, a bylaw clean-up, but real discussion began with Article 17, regarding Natick’s amendment of its zoning bylaws to comply with the updated floodplain map and make owners of property in certain flood zones eligible for flood insurance (cha-ching!). The presentation by Amanda Loomis, director of Community & Economic Development, and subsequent discussion on the motion under Article 17 lasted for half an hour.

Questions were raised on the Town Meeting floor about whether the town is alerting those who may now have to pay for flood insurance, but Natick Environmental Planner/Conservation Agent Claire Rundelli said Natick does not get involved in that, as this is the purview of the National Flood Insurance Program. Natick will make updated floodplain maps available on its website, and they are also available via the Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA).

“No, there currently is not a plan [by the town] for outreach,” she said, adding later that determining the flood elevation for individual properties would involve widespread topographical surveying that the town doesn’t have the resources to pay for. The town is happy to answer people’s questions and provide contact information for the flood insurance program, though, she said.

 

Town Meeting voted in favor of the motion by a count of 107-16-3.

Next up was Article 18, having to do with updates to the accessory dwelling unit (ADU) bylaw that sync up with a state law on ADUs that went into effect earlier this year. More specifically, the update addresses the gross floor area definition (Natick currently measures from the exterior wall in, while the state measures from the interior wall in). Natick had created an ADU bylaw last fall in anticipation of the state law going through.

The questions on ADUs seemed to have to do more with ADUs in general, than the specifics of the motion. These are new, so people are still trying to get their arms around what is and isn’t allowed. The impetus behind new freedom around ADUs is to help address the housing shortage, though relatively few ADUs have been built in the area to date from what we’ve heard.

The motion passed by a 116-10-1 vote.

Also under the zoning articles was #19, which focused on cleaning up the Natick zoning bylaw and map regarding indoor recreational businesses, say like mini-golf, personal training, or volleyball. Natick is booming with such businesses, from the Mall to Tech Circle, and this bylaw change would allow such enterprises in more areas (though limited to one-tenth of the lot).

Julian Munnich, a Town Meeting member as well as Finance Committee member, warned that supporting this motion amounts to a policy change under which the town kind of gives up on areas for industrial businesses, like MathWorks, in favor of entertainment businesses. He said: “Now if as a town we’re making a policy change saying, eh, that time when we used to create good paying jobs and have a tax base, we’re past that now. Now it’s more important for us to have skipping and dancing and ball throwing facilities. I mean those are important, but that isn’t what creates a tax base.”

Town Meeting voted in favor of the motion 105-22-1.

The last article to generate any notable discussion was Article 20, seeking to align the definition of body art establishments in the zoning bylaw with the Board of Health’s definition of body art (the Board regulates all operations that produce bloodborne pathogens). The article also aimed to align body art establishments with personal services businesses like salons, since their offerings have begun to overlap, Loomis said.

This article had moderator Alderson musing about a possible spa day if Town Meeting were to go another night.

Body art establishments have been in the zoning bylaw since 2001, when they “were looked upon very differently than they are today,” and in 2024 Fall Annual Town Meeting allowed body art businesses to operate in the downtown mixed-use area. Article 20 would allow such establishments in even more parts of town.

Town Meeting and Planning Board member Andy Meyer shared background that a local salon owner had come forward to town officials a while back to let them know he was at a competitive disadvantage to businesses in nearby communities that were allowed to offer body art and other salon services. Town bodies and leaders got together to propose changes to the zoning bylaw to support such businesses and remove “that inadvertent competitive disadvantage,” Meyer said.

Town Meeting voted in favor of the motion 115-8-1.

What’s left for this Thursday, May 8? Articles 23-25, the latter being a citizen petition on stabilization funds.

See an unofficial running list of Town Meeting votes on the Natick Financial Committee website.

Community commendable—thanks to Frank Foss for 18 years as Town Meeting moderator

May 2, 2025 by Admin

To the editor:

Spring Annual Town Meeting in Natick opened on Monday with a resolution from its membership thanking Frank Foss for his 18 years of service as our Town Moderator, as well as his 34 years of volunteer service on many municipal committees including 12 on the Finance Committee, with 4 as its chair, and many terms as a Town Meeting Member.

State Senate President Karen Spilka cleared her schedule to personally present Frank with a legislative citation and offer words of gratitude for Frank’s many accomplishments which included the transition to electronic voting, institution of new member orientations, and navigating the logistics of moderating remote town meetings during the pandemic. Representative David Linsky was unfortunately not able to be present, but new moderator Jeff Alderson read Linsky’s citation into the record. Town Meeting presented Frank with a resolution that was read by retired Town Clerk Diane Packer.

If you know Frank, you know that he loves town meeting and judiciously protected the right of people to have their voices heard, as a good moderator should. He was unafraid to gavel someone out of order for speaking out of turn or being repetitious. I was gaveled on occasion, even Mrs. Foss.

As an entrepreneur and principal of Foss Enterprises, Frank generously shared his broad swathe of professional experiences in telecommunications, broadband, community development, parliamentary procedure, property management, government and team leadership. It was a joy to celebrate his service. Thank you Frank Foss for giving Town Meeting 18 years as our moderator.

Respectfully,

Donna M. McKenzie, MDiv, PhD
Town Meeting Member, Precinct 8

Natick Spring Annual Town Meeting opening night recap: Un-stabilization; A Happy Fuss over Foss; Override-infused budget

May 1, 2025 by Bob Brown

New Natick Town Moderator Jeff Alderson kicked off Spring Annual Town Meeting on April 29 by heading into the Wayback Machine, citing the 250th anniversary of the American Revolution as a way of providing historical perspective on the legislative tradition. “In our current practice of local participatory democracy, we maintain and renew a practice of self-governance,” he began, crediting the Natick Historical Society and Massachusetts Moderators Association for their help on the statement.

The statement also provided a smooth segue to swearing in newly elected Town Meeting members. As Alderson said, “By gathering together today, we honor and embrace the town meetings of 250 years ago…”

Thus began the pomp and circumstance portion of Town Meeting, leading into the discussion of articles on the warrant. For this night, that mainly meant the Omnibus Budget. Below is our summary of the proceedings, but see the Natick Pegasus recording if you want to watch the whole thing.)

 

Hey, are we the only media outlet covering Natick’s Town Meeting? Please consider supporting your dedicated Natick news source with a tax-deductible donation

 

Stabilization funds

Speaking of the Revolutionary War… the first shot fired at this Town Meeting came from member Bruce Evans, also a Select Board member, before the session was 30 minutes old. Evans rose to oppose a motion by member Paul Griesmer to move a citizen petition on stabilization funds (Article 25) in front of Article 7, also on stabilization funds. Griesmer said it would make sense to discuss the related items together, but Evans took exception to this.

“The reason I don’t think the procedure is necessary at this time is this is an end run to try to circumvent a budget, and I will not support it,” Evans said.

This take was foreshadowed at a Select Board meeting earlier this month during which the Board voted for indefinite postponement of Article 25.

Town Meeting member Kathryn Coughlin, a Select Board colleague of Evans, rose to say there might be no point to moving 25 ahead of 7 because even if Town Meeting approved the motion under Article 25 it wouldn’t become effective until seven days later.

Former moderator and current Town Meeting member Frank Foss raised a point of order on this, arguing that Coughlin was making a substantive point, not a procedural one. Alderson agreed, explaining there was no procedural issue with moving 25 ahead of 7.

Griesmer then returned to the mic and said Article 25 is a “long-run financial planning or policy issue around what stabilization funds are for. It has nothing to do with the override, it has nothing to do with the omnibus budget, and it is not an end run around anything…”

Town Meeting voted 33-89-3, so the motion didn’t pass.

Making a bit of a Fuss over Foss

The introduction of Alderson as moderator marked the end of Frank Foss’s tenure in the post. Foss was recognized near the start of the night for his 18 years as moderator. Alderson had hoped to do so before Foss introduced himself through that earlier point of order, “kind of setting the tone for the rest of my tenure as moderator,” Alderson quipped to laughter.

The new moderator went on to read a citation for Foss on behalf of State Rep. David Linsky, who could not be there in person. Senate President Karen Spilka was on hand to read a citation from the Senate. Retired Town Clerk Diane Packer also read a resolution that included numerous accolades for Foss: “He was very instrumental in encouraging and implementing electronic voting; he started the annual new Town Meeting member orientation program; hosted a Pegasus show before every session of Town Meeting to inform the citizenry of the issues before the meeting; and he was absolutely critical in setting up the process and procedures that allowed and facilitated on-line Town Meeting during the pandemic…”

In sharing his thoughts, Foss recalled how people he spoke to before running for public office gave him similar advice that boiled down to: “If you’re going to run for office, there’s absolutely nothing in your town that you’re going to do yourself. You’re going to be doing it with a lot of other people with a lot of help…” Plus: Listen to all the voices before making decisions, he was told.

The Omnibus Budget

After whizzing through a couple of perfunctory articles, it was on to Article 3: The Omnibus Budget. This is the roughly $202 million in spending requested to run the town over the next fiscal year. Discussion of three motions under Article 3 took up the last hour-and-a-half of the first night’s session.

Town Meeting voted at the start of discussion on this article for an amendment having to do with using free cash to cover a revenue shortfall.

Then a series of speakers addressed portions of the budget related to their organizations or departments. This included the superintendents of Natick Public Schools and Keefe Regional Technical School (more than 60 Natick students attend Keefe Tech), as well as the town’s administration and finance team.

As Town Administrator Jamie Errickson stated at the start of presentations and discussion on the budget, the recent debate and ultimate vote for the $7M override shaped the omnibus budget put forth to Town Meeting. The budget, he said, “is a step toward addressing the town’s structural deficit” (i.e., expenses outpacing revenues) and preserves the town’s stabilization funds “to ensure the long-term health of the town,” especially in light of broader economic flux. What’s more, the budget supports the town and school’s ability to provide services “at exceptional value,” he said.

Errickson then handed the baton to Deputy Town Administrator & Director of Finance John Townsend, who gave a high-level overview of the budget. As noted, the bulk of revenue raised by the town comes from property taxes, with state aid and local receipts (motor vehicle and restaurant excise taxes, permits, etc.) making up most of the rest. This marks the first year since the pandemic in which the town does not have COVID relief funds from the feds to bolster its budget. As always, schools account for the biggest chunk of expenses, at $95M-plus; in addition, schools account for more than half of the roughly $59M in shared services (healthcare expenses, etc.).

Natick had prepped budgets ahead of the recent town election based on the override being approved or not. The “no override” budget would have included $6.5M in free cash to cover expenses—that would be a way higher amount of free cash used for operating costs than is typical or recommended (free cash is that money left over at the end of a fiscal year after actual expenses). As is, the $3.4M in free cash Natick budgeted for is a bit higher than preferred, Townsend said.

(Looking at Townsend’s presentation, you’ll see Omnibus Budget revenue and expenses at nearly $209 million apiece. These larger Omnibus Budget figures take into consideration $6.7M plus in “below the line items” that include some capital expenses and Other Post-Employment Benefits Trust Fund contributions.)

Natick Public Schools Supt. Dr. Melissa Spash went over the $95M-plus school budget request, up 10% from the previous year. If any of what Spash said sounded familiar, that might be because the school team has held 19 public meetings related to the budget this year.

The drivers behind the increase, she said, were renegotiated contracts with staff (accounting for about half of the budget increase), plus rising mandatory special education tuition costs and transportation costs. It also includes the replacement of one-time funds used in FY25. Spash reminded Town Meeting about $2M in cost reductions made by the schools over the past two years, including cuts of ten full-time equivalent positions for the coming fiscal year.

“In summary, this is a stabilization budget, not an expansion budget…,” she said. “We balance unavoidable increases with strategic cuts, and we honor commitments to both our staff and community.”

Following Spash’s presentation, the moderator opened the floor to questions and comments.

Town Meeting member David Coffey asked clarifying questions about upcoming water/sewer charge increases and Chapter 70 education funding, and after having those addressed, commented that “my biggest regret, in debate, is that we’re not allowed the opportunity to deep dive into each department’s budget individually. Rather, we’re given a $200 million lump sum budget and we’re told, ‘take it or leave it’…” (A similar sentiment was shared recently at Wellesley’s Annual Town Meeting, where a member proposed splitting out the school budget…that motion failed.)

Town Meeting member Griesmer sought clarification on the town’s efforts to collect back taxes, which he said could net Natick around $15 million. “It’s good that we’re getting after it,” he said.

Dr. Spash was asked about how the Natick Public Schools system uses comparative data for other districts in coming up with its compensation offerings. Town Meeting member Dan Sullivan urged NPS and the School Committee to focus on comparing itself not to districts across the state, but rather those bordering Natick and those bordering those communities. He said the regional data he’s looked at shows Natick toward the bottom of compensation rankings in recent years. “We put a lot of pride in our school system and we want to have the very best educators. When someone is looking for a region to come to, it would be my hope that Natick would be a desirable community from a compensation perspective,” he said.

Splash also answered a question about how the budget matches up with the number of students. She said enrollment is roughly flat, but that the number of students with disabilities and the number of English language learners are going up (as is true across the state). Those students require different approaches to teaching, such as in smaller groups, and that leads to higher costs.

The discussion and debate was relatively brief, and Town Meeting approved Motion A for the budget appropriation by a count of 116-7.

Motion B under Article 3 covered a $22M-plus budget for town enterprise funds for water/sewer and the Sassamon Trace Golf Course. Motion B was approved by a vote of 117-2. Motion C also had to do with a Golf Course appropriation, and it passed easily.

Up next, on Thursday, May 1, Town Meeting tackles warrant Articles 15, 5 through 14, and 16 through 25, as time allows. Article 15 involves appropriation of Community Preservation Act (CPA) administrative funds.

Before Thursday night’s over, Town Meeting will return to the subject of stabilization funds that briefly earned the floor’s attention on opening night.

Time to prep for Natick Spring Annual Town Meeting

April 14, 2025 by Admin

Natick Spring Annual Town Meeting starts on April 29 at Kennedy Middle School, but this is the week to get yourself set up for the action whether or not you are a Town Meeting member. (A procedural session will take place on April 22, but you don’t need to go.)

An orientation session is being held for Town Meeting members on April 15 at 6pm at Kennedy Middle School. This is a good opportunity for new and existing members to meet others in their precinct.

The session will go over the auditorium layout, order of opening ceremonies, and the general flow of the meeting. Rules and procedures will be reviewed, and new members will get a chance to practice voting, make points of order, and introduce motions on the floor.

New and existing Town Meeting members are also encouraged to review the handbook and Finance Committee recommendations. We previewed the warrant of articles last month.

Town Moderator Jeff Alderson also encourages Town Meeting members and other members of the public interested in following along to sign up for email or text alerts related to Town Meeting via the town website. This way, you can stay abreast of the Town Meeting agenda and be alerted to any new motions.
